# Codec: building Track B

**Period:** May 2026
**Status:** five-round optimization pass complete, baseline established

## The step this thread contributed

Earlier work in Project Continua focused on the receiver side: can a
mathematics-using listener derive a primitive's meaning from the audio
alone? Track B asks the engineering question that runs parallel to that:
given the same math-native vocabulary, how much data per second can the
channel actually carry, and how does that throughput hold up as the
channel gets worse?

For the meaning-discarding bandwidth comparison (how much data per
second can the channel carry if you don't constrain the symbols to be
self-describing), see [Acoustic bandwidth: a baseline](/research/bandwidth).
The two pages are companion measurements: this page studies the
math-native codec, that page studies the unconstrained OFDM ceiling,
and the gap between them quantifies the cost of meaning-preservation.

This page is the record of a five-round optimization pass on a composed
codec built on top of the v4 primitives. Each round adds one standard
technique from communications engineering and measures what it buys us.
The goal is an honest baseline for the conversation that follows, not a
tuned product.

## How the codec works

The baseline encodes a byte payload by chopping its bitstream into 3-bit
groups, mapping each group to one of eight RATIO primitives (the most
noise-robust primitive in the per-primitive benchmark), and rendering
them in SEQUENCE with a fixed silence gap between symbols so the decoder
can find the boundaries. The decoder segments the audio at those gaps,
identifies each block's RATIO from its spectral peaks, and reassembles
the bitstream. The math-native vocabulary is doing the actual carrying;
the codec is a thin protocol wrapper around it.

That baseline carries about **1.94 bits per second** on a clean channel,
with a sharp noise cliff at roughly +15 dB SNR. The five rounds below
trade between two axes: lowering that noise cliff and raising the
clean-channel throughput.

## Round 1: symbol repetition with majority vote

Encode each symbol three times in a row. The decoder takes a majority
vote across the three copies. Spends a factor of three in throughput to
gain noise immunity, on the same principle that a vote of three noisy
witnesses beats one. Direct, well-understood, easy to compose with
anything that comes later.

**Contribution:** establishes the noise-floor / throughput trade-off
explicitly, with the simplest possible mechanism. The result was
sobering: the cliff against additive noise sits at the same +15 dB SNR
as baseline, because at that cliff multiple consecutive symbols
corrupt simultaneously and the majority vote inherits the corruption
from all three copies. Repetition does buy a few dB in reverb but at
1/3 the throughput cost.

## Round 2: multi-band parallel symbols

Render two independent RATIO dyads in disjoint frequency bands at the
same time: one in 100 to 700 Hz, one in 900 to 3600 Hz. Each symbol
slot now carries six bits instead of three. The decoder extracts the
ratio in each band separately. The vocabulary table is the same;
each band uses the same eight RATIOs after octave-shifting them into
its assigned spectral range, so the operator's meaning (the ratio) is
preserved while the actual peak frequencies move.

**Contribution:** demonstrates that the channel has room for parallel
symbol streams in disjoint spectral bands. Doubles raw clean-channel
throughput from 1.94 to 3.66 bits per second with no loss of noise
robustness in reverb, and matching the baseline cliff in additive
noise (no worse, no better).

## Round 3: Hamming(7,4) forward error correction

Apply a textbook Hamming(7,4) code to the bitstream before symbol
mapping. Every four data bits become seven channel bits with a
single-bit error correction capability. Costs four sevenths of the raw
throughput; buys back any cell where exactly one channel bit per
seven-bit codeword was flipped.

**Contribution:** introduces explicit error correction as a separate
layer, isolating "did the symbol decode wrong" from "is the payload
recoverable." On its own Hamming(7,4) does not push the additive-noise
cliff, because at the cliff multiple bits per codeword corrupt and the
single-bit window is exceeded. The interesting result was when
Hamming was stacked with pilot calibration: the combination produced
the lowest reverb floor in the entire bake-off.

<img src="/codec/codec_round_R3.png" alt="Round 3: Hamming(7,4) vs baseline" className="my-6 rounded border border-zinc-200 dark:border-zinc-800" />

## Round 4: pilot tone and adaptive decoder

Prepend a short reference tone of known frequency and amplitude
(220 Hz, 0.4 seconds) before the payload. The decoder measures its
spectral peak and uses the pilot-to-noise ratio to set its symbol
identification threshold adaptively rather than assuming a fixed
fraction of block peak. Borrowed from broadcast radio practice, where a
pilot subcarrier in FM stereo serves the same calibration role.

**Contribution:** adds a small overhead per message but lets the decoder
ground its decisions in measured channel conditions rather than fixed
constants. Modest robustness improvement on its own; the meaningful
effect is compositional, particularly when stacked with Hamming.

<img src="/codec/codec_round_R4.png" alt="Round 4: pilot tone vs baseline" className="my-6 rounded border border-zinc-200 dark:border-zinc-800" />

## Round 5: bake-off

The four rounds compose. Configurations were swept across white noise,
pink noise, and a small-room reverb (RT60 about 0.35 seconds), at SNR
steps from +30 dB down to -15 dB. The chart below shows effective bits
per second versus SNR for each noise type. Effective bits per second is
defined as the raw rate scaled down by the bit error rate, so any cell
above zero is doing useful work.

## Reading the results

The headline numbers, clean-channel:

- **Multi-band** lifts throughput to **3.66 bits per second**, almost
  exactly double the baseline 1.94. The doubling holds across every
  noise type at high SNR and survives down to where the decoder cliff
  hits, so this is a clean win on the throughput axis with no obvious
  cost.
- Multi-band with the pilot tone calibration is **3.30 bps**, a small
  overhead that buys back a few dB of noise-floor improvement in pink
  noise.
- Multi-band stacked with Hamming(7,4) and pilot lands at **2.01 bps**.
  The Hamming overhead approximately halves the multi-band gain, but
  this configuration delivers the lowest reverb floor of any
  multi-band config and matches the baseline throughput.

The noise-axis findings were less symmetric than expected, and that
turned out to be the more useful result:

- **Reverb is consistently the friendliest noise** for every
  configuration. The reverb chart above shows every line holding its
  clean-channel throughput essentially flat all the way down to -15 dB
  direct-to-reverberant ratio. Late echoes smear the signal in time
  without raising the broadband noise floor that the spectral decoder
  thresholds against. Real rooms should not threaten this codec.
- **Additive white and pink noise produce a sharp cliff at +15 to +30 dB
  SNR** that none of the simple techniques in this pass actually
  pushed down meaningfully. Symbol repetition voted three corrupted
  copies into one corrupted symbol; Hamming(7,4) corrects single-bit
  errors per codeword, but at the cliff multiple bits flip in the same
  seven-bit window and Hamming's correction window is exceeded. The
  cliff is a feature of the channel-symbol structure, not of any
  particular FEC's strength.
- **The clean exception is reverb plus Hamming plus pilot calibration,
  which holds 1% bit error rate all the way down to -15 dB.** Reverb-
  induced errors are sparse and well-spaced enough that Hamming can
  catch them; the pilot tone keeps the symbol identifier well-
  calibrated as the diffuse energy rises.

The honest read is that this pass landed two of the four goals: the
throughput axis moved cleanly (multi-band, two parallel streams), and
the codec turned out to be naturally reverb-robust. The noise-floor
axis against broadband additive noise was not improved by any of the
simple techniques tried here. That points at the next pass: stronger
forward error correction with a longer error-correcting window, or
soft-decision decoding that uses spectral peak confidence as a per-bit
likelihood rather than a hard symbol decision.

A few things worth noticing while listening. The clean signal is the
pulse-train of symbols you hear plainly. At +20 dB white noise you
can still pick out the rhythm under the hiss. At +10 dB white, the
hiss has swallowed the pulses; the decoder picks essentially random
peaks and returns garbage. The two reverb clips at +10 and 0 dB sound
audibly wetter than the clean version but the rhythm and pitch
content survive intact, which is exactly what the throughput charts
above predicted. The -10 dB reverb is almost all room and only some
of the payload makes it through; the partial decode `.on.in.a` from
`continua` is the kind of corruption a single-bit FEC would clean up.

## Future work in this thread

- **Stronger error correction with a longer window.** Hamming(7,4)
  corrects a single bit per seven-bit codeword, which is too narrow
  for the burst errors that appear at the additive-noise cliff.
  Reed-Solomon or a convolutional code with Viterbi decoding has a
  much longer correction window and should be the next move on the
  noise-floor axis.
- **Soft-decision decoding.** Right now each symbol is identified
  with a hard decision (one of eight ratios). The spectrum carries
  more information than that: peak height, peak sharpness, presence of
  spurious peaks. Treating each symbol's identification as a
  likelihood vector instead of a single guess would feed a soft-input
  decoder and push the cliff down further at no throughput cost.
- **MP3 and lossy compression as a noise type.** Real audio channels
  often include perceptual codecs. This pass skipped it only because
  it would add an ffmpeg dependency.
- **Narrowband jammers.** RATIO is the noise champion against
  broadband interference. Narrowband jammers aimed at its peaks
  would be the natural stress test of the spectral approach.
- **Joint Track A and Track B.** Render noisy codec messages and let
  human listeners try to decode them through the v4 dashboard. This
  joins the pedagogy and engineering tracks in a single measurement:
  what is the human equivalent of a 1% bit error rate, and at what
  SNR does the human curve cross the algorithmic one?
