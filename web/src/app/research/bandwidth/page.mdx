# Acoustic bandwidth: a baseline

**Period:** May 2026
**Status:** baseline established, comparison to math-native codec in place

## The question this thread asks

How much data per second can be carried by a sound wave? Not the
meaning-preserving question that the math-native codec asks, but the
raw engineering one: ignoring any constraint about what the audio
"means," what's the channel actually capable of?

This page is the bandwidth baseline. It exists to put the math-native
codec's numbers in perspective: when the math-native codec carries 1.94
bits per second on a clean channel, is that close to the physical
ceiling for audio data, or far below it? Without a baseline, we cannot
tell.

## The physical ceiling

Shannon and Hartley gave the answer in 1949. For a channel of
bandwidth `B` (in Hertz) and signal-to-noise ratio `S/N`:

> **C = B × log₂(1 + S/N)** bits per second

The audible band is roughly **20 kHz wide**. At a moderate SNR of
30 dB (signal-to-noise ratio of 1000:1):

> C ≈ 20,000 × log₂(1001) ≈ **200 kbps**

That is the absolute physical ceiling for an audio data channel
in clean conditions. It is the number we are working toward when we
ask "how high can we push the bandwidth axis."

Comparison points from the engineering literature:

- **V.92 dial-up modem**: 56 kbps over a 3 kHz audio-bandwidth phone
  line. Within an order of magnitude of the Shannon ceiling for that
  narrow band.
- **DTMF (touch-tone telephone)**: ~10 bps, ~3 kHz bandwidth, designed
  for human-comprehensible reliability over very noisy phone lines.
- **Chirp.io / Google Nearby (audio data sharing)**: ~1-2 kbps, full
  audio band, designed for short-range broadcast through speakers and
  mics in noisy real-world environments.
- **Math-native codec (this project, baseline)**:
  [1.94 bps clean, 3.66 bps with multi-band](/research/codec). Many
  orders of magnitude below the ceiling, because the symbol vocabulary
  is designed for derivability rather than throughput.

## The design

To put a concrete number against the ceiling, we built a straightforward
OFDM (orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing) encoder/decoder. OFDM
is the textbook approach for packing maximum bits into a band: divide
the spectrum into many narrow subcarriers and modulate each one
independently. It is what WiFi, 4G LTE, 5G, DVB-T, and most modern
audio data systems use.

Our configuration:

- **64 subcarriers** spaced 50 Hz apart, from 200 Hz to 3350 Hz
- Three modulation schemes per subcarrier:
  - **BPSK** (binary phase-shift keying): 1 bit per subcarrier, encoded
    as phase 0 or pi
  - **QPSK** (quadrature phase-shift keying): 2 bits per subcarrier,
    encoded in the four quadrants of the complex plane with Gray coding
  - **16-QAM** (16-point quadrature amplitude modulation): 4 bits per
    subcarrier, encoded as a 4x4 grid of in-phase and quadrature
    amplitudes
- **100 ms symbol duration**, no inter-symbol gap
- Carrier frequencies aligned to the FFT bin grid (every 5th bin at 50
  Hz spacing) so per-carrier phase rotation is zero and QPSK / QAM
  decoding survives without timing-recovery scaffolding
- Hanning window for spectral leakage suppression
- No FEC, no cyclic prefix, no timing sync

Raw rates: BPSK **640 bps**, QPSK **1280 bps**, 16-QAM **2560 bps**.

The implementation is one file (`continua/codec_ofdm.py`), about 300
lines including the three constellation mappers. The design priority
was clarity of comparison, not absolute performance. A production
audio modem with stronger FEC and a longer-window code would carry
several kilobits per second reliably; this is the simplest honest
OFDM.

## Results

The benchmark sweeps BER (bit error rate) across the same noise types
used for the math-native codec: additive white, additive pink, and
small-room algorithmic reverb at RT60 about 0.35 seconds, for each
modulation scheme.

| Scheme | Raw bps | White 1%-BER floor | Pink 1%-BER floor | Reverb 1%-BER floor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| BPSK   | 640  | -5 dB SNR | -5 dB SNR | +5 dB |
| QPSK   | 1280 | -5 dB SNR | 0 dB SNR  | +10 dB |
| 16-QAM | 2560 | +30 dB SNR | +30 dB SNR | (none in sweep) |

The constellation density-vs-margin trade is the central engineering
finding here. Each step from BPSK to QPSK to 16-QAM doubles raw
throughput but costs noise margin. BPSK and QPSK are almost identical
in robustness because their constellation points are equally distant
from the origin (the only thing the demodulator has to detect under
noise is sign); 16-QAM crowds 16 points into the same complex plane
and any of the four amplitude levels can be misread when noise is
above a few dB of constellation spacing.

A few observations worth surfacing:

- **BPSK at 640 bps clean is about 330x the math-native codec's 1.94 bps.
  QPSK and 16-QAM stretch the gap to 660x and 1320x respectively.**
  The cost of meaning-preservation is two to three orders of magnitude
  of throughput, depending on which OFDM scheme you compare against.
  That cost is what we are buying when we use the math-native
  vocabulary.
- **OFDM has graceful BER degradation, no sharp cliff.** Where the
  math-native codec falls off a cliff at +15 dB additive noise
  (going from 0% BER to 50% in 5 dB), BPSK OFDM's BER rises smoothly:
  0% at +5 dB, 6% at -5 dB, 13% at -10 dB. The graceful curve comes
  from the per-subcarrier independence; only the worst-noise
  subcarriers flip while the rest continue to decode.
- **OFDM is reverb-fragile where the math-native codec was reverb-friendly.**
  This is the most interesting finding in the comparison. Reverb
  scrambles inter-symbol phase relationships, which is exactly what
  OFDM's BPSK demodulation depends on; the math-native codec
  identifies symbols spectrally rather than by phase and is therefore
  immune. BPSK OFDM needs +5 dB reverb to stay below 1% BER; 16-QAM
  is fundamentally broken in reverb even at +30 dB direct-to-reverb
  ratio (6.7% BER at the cleanest setting).

The two designs make exactly opposite trades. OFDM trades meaning for
throughput, and the constellation choice trades phase-based robustness
for broadband-noise-graceful degradation. The math-native codec trades
throughput for meaning, and the spectral-identification choice trades
phase-based throughput for reverb robustness. They are complementary,
not competing.

(512 bits). For payloads that fill the constellation exactly the
quoted theoretical rates are what you get.

## How this relates to the math-native codec

The math-native codec at [/research/codec](/research/codec) is the
project's research output along the meaning-preserving axis. The
bandwidth baseline on this page is a comparator, not a competitor.
Two questions are being asked in parallel:

1. **How much data per second can be carried** by a sound wave (this
   page).
2. **How much of that throughput can be preserved while keeping the
   audio's mathematical structure self-describing** (the math-native
   codec page).

The gap between the two is the cost of meaning-preservation in
acoustic data, quantified. Today that gap ranges from 330x (math-
native baseline 1.94 bps vs OFDM BPSK 640 bps) to 1320x (vs OFDM
16-QAM 2560 bps). A future direction is a hybrid that layers OFDM in
the upper spectrum on top of math-native primitives in the lower
spectrum: the math-native lower band carries the self-describing
bootstrap layer that a fresh receiver can derive from physics and
arithmetic alone; the OFDM upper band carries high-throughput payload
once the receiver has decoded the bootstrap. This would preserve the
meaning-bearing claim while bringing the throughput close to the
ceiling.

## Future work in this thread

- **QPSK or 16-QAM**: doubles or quadruples bits per subcarrier at
  the cost of additional noise sensitivity. Brings the channel into
  the kilobit-per-second range.
- **Forward error correction**: a Reed-Solomon or convolutional code
  on top of OFDM pulls the SNR floor down several dB.
- **Pilot subcarriers for timing sync**: real OFDM systems include
  a few subcarriers carrying a known pattern so the receiver can lock
  on without being told the exact symbol boundary. This work assumes
  perfect sync; pilots remove that assumption.
- **Hybrid math-native + OFDM**: math-native primitives in the lower
  spectrum (rhythm, intervals, glides, audible structure) carrying
  the self-describing layer; OFDM in the upper spectrum carrying bulk
  payload. The receiver decodes the math-native layer first to
  bootstrap, then uses the OFDM layer for high-throughput data.
